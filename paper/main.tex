\begin{abstract}
Bus holding control requires reinforcement learning (RL) policies that generalize from limited real-world logs to new traffic conditions without expensive online interaction. We benchmark four families of methods on a SUMO microscopic simulation of a real bus route with 389 vehicles and 12 lines, trained from 60{,}000 offline transitions recorded under a heuristic controller. Our comparison includes (i) pure offline methods: Behavior Cloning and an H2O+ AWR baseline; (ii) pure online Soft Actor-Critic (SAC); and (iii) two state-of-the-art offline-to-online approaches, WSRL \citep{zhou2024efficient} and RLPD \citep{ball2023efficient}. We also introduce \textbf{RE-SAC offline}, a pure offline algorithm that combines an ensemble of ten Q-networks with Lower-Confidence-Bound (LCB) pessimism as an epistemic regularizer; an ablation shows that adding an $L_1$ weight-norm penalty does not further improve results, so the effective recipe is simply ``ensemble + LCB''. Using a rigorous post-hoc evaluation protocol (10 SUMO episodes per checkpoint across three seeds), we find: (1) naive offline methods overfit sharply\,---\,BC and H2O+ offline both peak early then degrade to $-9439$ and $-9482$ respectively by the end of training; (2) offline-to-online methods attain stable final returns of $-4552$ to $-5008$; (3) Online SAC attains only $-6572$ and exhibits value-function instability under extended training; and (4) \textbf{RE-SAC offline achieves $-4267 \pm 140$, the best result overall, without any online environment interaction}. Doubling SAC's training budget from 300 to 600 epochs does not close the gap, confirming that the advantage is algorithmic rather than budget-driven. We release all code, data, and 151 evaluated checkpoints.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Public-transit bus holding\,---\,delaying a bus at a stop to restore headway uniformity\,---\,is a canonical real-world control problem plagued by \emph{bus bunching}: small perturbations propagate and cluster buses, degrading service quality \citep{daganzo2009headway,bartholdi2012self}. Reinforcement learning has been applied to bus holding \citep{alesiani2018reinforcement,wang2020holding}, but deploying on-policy training on a real bus line is operationally risky and slow. \emph{Offline} RL and \emph{offline-to-online} fine-tuning are natural fits: a fleet of buses generates operational logs continuously, and new policies can be trained without incurring the cost of exploratory interaction \citep{levine2020offline}.

The offline-to-online literature offers two dominant recipes. \textbf{Warm-start RL (WSRL)} \citep{zhou2024efficient} pre-trains a policy on offline data and discards the data for online fine-tuning, arguing that extended use of offline data creates distributional mismatch. \textbf{RLPD} \citep{ball2023efficient} initializes a policy from scratch and samples half-and-half from offline and online buffers throughout training, relying on simple data mixing. Both have been validated on D4RL and Adroit, where online interactions are cheap. On real-world transit tasks, however, online interaction is \emph{not} cheap: each training episode consumes simulator time equivalent to an entire peak-hour operational window.

This raises the central question of this paper: \textbf{do offline-to-online methods actually deliver value over well-regularized pure offline training for transit RL?} We answer negatively. On a production-scale SUMO simulation of a real bus network, we show that a pure offline algorithm with proper uncertainty regularization\,---\,which we call \emph{RE-SAC offline}\,---\,matches or exceeds all tested offline-to-online methods while requiring zero online rollouts. Both of the popular offline-to-online methods (WSRL, RLPD) reach a similar return ceiling, but none surpass RE-SAC's pure-offline performance.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A reproducible SUMO benchmark for offline and offline-to-online RL on real bus-holding data, with 60{,}000 real-world transitions from Line 7X (Changsha) and a calibrated microsimulation of the full 12-line network.
  \item \textbf{RE-SAC offline}, which combines (i) ensemble-Q Lower-Confidence-Bound (LCB) pessimism as an epistemic regularizer and (ii) an $L_1$ norm on the per-ensemble Q-network weights as an aleatoric regularizer, yielding the best overall performance.
  \item A rigorous post-hoc evaluation protocol: every saved checkpoint is re-evaluated on 10 SUMO episodes across three seeds (151 checkpoints total), exposing that during-training ``best'' metrics reported by existing offline-to-online work can overstate performance by up to 30\%.
  \item An extended online SAC training study (600 epochs) that demonstrates pure online RL cannot close the gap to RE-SAC offline even with doubled budget: it plateaus around $-6300$ and exhibits transient peaks from value-function instability rather than steady convergence.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Offline RL.} Offline RL learns from fixed logs without online interaction, addressing extrapolation error via conservatism \citep{kumar2020conservative}, implicit value regression \citep{kostrikov2022offline}, or ensemble pessimism. Our RE-SAC method draws on the Randomized Ensembled Double Q-learning (REDQ) \citep{chen2021randomized} architecture and the robust offline RL of \citet{yang2022rorl}, who use Q-function ensembles to enforce pessimism through lower-confidence bounds.

\paragraph{Offline-to-Online Fine-Tuning.} WSRL \citep{zhou2024efficient} argues that retaining offline data during online fine-tuning causes the replay buffer to drift and degrade, so the offline data should be discarded after warm-start. RLPD \citep{ball2023efficient} takes the opposite view: sample 50/50 from offline and online data throughout training; the offline data stabilizes Q-function updates. Cal-QL \citep{nakamoto2023calql} proposes calibration on top of conservative Q-learning for offline-to-online transfer. H2O/H2O+ \citep{niu2022trust} tackles sim-to-real by learning a dynamics discriminator between simulator and real logs; we include its offline-only mode (AWR-based) as the ``H2O+ offline'' baseline.

\paragraph{Bus Holding.} Headway-based holding \citep{daganzo2009headway,bartholdi2012self} is the classical control-theoretic baseline. Early RL approaches \citep{alesiani2018reinforcement,wang2020holding} train tabular or simple deep policies on analytical simulators without real data. Our benchmark uses a microsimulation calibrated against real passenger OD matrices and vehicle routes.

\section{Problem Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Simulator: SUMO Microscopic Traffic Simulation}
\label{sec:sumo}

\paragraph{SUMO overview.} Eclipse SUMO (Simulation of Urban MObility) version 1.25 \citep{lopez2018microscopic} is an open-source, space-continuous, time-discrete microscopic traffic simulator. Every vehicle is individually modeled with a car-following model (Krauss-based by default) and a lane-changing model (LC2013), both of which respect vehicle class, desired speed, headway preferences, and driver imperfection. Intersections use explicit right-of-way rules and fixed-time traffic-signal plans. We drive the simulator through \texttt{libsumo}, an in-process C++ API that bypasses the TraCI socket protocol and is roughly 10$\times$ faster than TraCI for the dense-observation regime we need. The simulator step length is set to 0.5\,s.

\paragraph{Changsha bus network.} Our simulation recreates the full operational environment of a 12-line bus network in Changsha, China. The network was calibrated against the real city topology and real passenger OD survey data. Its size and composition are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:sumo_stats} and its spatial layout is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:network}. Concretely, the road network contains 7{,}633 directed edges and 2{,}527 junctions, of which 246 carry programmed traffic signals. Buses, social vehicles, and pedestrians are all first-class agents: bus vehicles (\texttt{vClass=bus}, 50-passenger capacity, 4\,s per-stop boarding time) share road edges with $\sim$98{,}000 social vehicle trips across two scenario files ($c_1$: dense weekday traffic, 61{,}044 trips; $c_2$: lighter weekday traffic with added bypass routes, 37{,}644 trips), and service 15{,}079 simulated passengers whose trip plans are drawn from OD matrices calibrated against transit card data (14{,}211 regular workday trips plus 868 inter-line transfer trips).

\paragraph{Traffic and passenger loads.} Every episode begins by loading five SUMO route files: the bus timetable (\texttt{d\_bus\_rou/2\_bus\_timetable}), two social-vehicle demand files that are concatenated to form the background traffic, and two passenger demand files for main workday travel and cross-line transfers. Bus departure times follow the real timetable for that line and direction, so the 50 Line 7X buses enter the simulation at realistic headways. Social vehicles sampled from the same distribution also introduce episode-to-episode variability, since their precise departure offsets, routes, and lane choices differ across seeds. The combined result is that evaluation returns have a genuine simulator-driven standard deviation of roughly 2000\,---\,about 30\% of the mean\,---\,which is why we insist on $N=10$ episodes per post-hoc evaluation (Sec.~\ref{sec:protocol}).

\paragraph{Agent-controlled line.} Following convention in the bus-holding literature \citep{wang2020holding}, we single out one line for learning while letting the other 11 follow their heuristic timetable. We use Line 7X, the longest route in the network (13.1\,km, 25 stops, 50 buses per day) as our agent-controlled line. Line 7X's mate in the reverse direction (7S) is \emph{not} agent-controlled; its buses follow the baseline schedule. All 338 buses on the other 5 line pairs (102, 311, 122, 406, 705) likewise run under their heuristic schedule and serve to generate realistic traffic and bus-to-bus interactions.

\subsection{MDP Formulation}
\label{sec:mdp}

Each Line 7X bus becomes an agent at the moment it reaches a station and requests a holding decision. The bus is passive during travel; decisions are only requested at the 25 scheduled stops along its route.

\paragraph{Observation.} The observation $o \in \mathbb{R}^{17}$ consists of 5 categorical and 12 continuous features. The categorical features (line, bus, station, time-period, direction) index learned embedding tables. The continuous features include: preceding-bus headway $h^{-}$ (s), following-bus headway $h^{+}$ (s), scheduled-vs-actual arrival lag, current station dwell-time budget, normalized route progress $\in [0, 1]$, current occupancy fraction $\in [0, 1]$, predicted boarding queue at the stop, station-level traffic density, predicted travel time to next stop, headway rolling mean and variance over the past 3 stops, and a local bunching indicator. All continuous features are normalized using statistics computed from the offline data.

\paragraph{Action.} The action $a = (a_{\text{hold}}, a_{\text{speed}}) \in [-1, 1]^2$ is produced by a Tanh-Gaussian policy. The first dimension is mapped to a hold duration via $t_{\text{hold}} = \text{clip}(30 \cdot a_{\text{hold}} + 30,\, 0,\, 60)$ seconds; the bus waits at the stop for $t_{\text{hold}}$ before departing. The second dimension is mapped to a post-stop speed multiplier using a Bang-Bang 5-tier discretization:
\[
\mu_{\text{speed}} = \begin{cases}
1.2 & a_{\text{speed}} > 0.6 \\
1.1 & 0.2 < a_{\text{speed}} \le 0.6 \\
1.0 & -0.2 \le a_{\text{speed}} \le 0.2 \\
0.9 & -0.6 \le a_{\text{speed}} < -0.2 \\
0.8 & a_{\text{speed}} < -0.6
\end{cases}
\]
applied as a multiplier on the bus's default desired speed until it reaches the next stop. This design lets the agent slow down a fast-moving leader or speed up a lagging follower without breaking SUMO's lane-changing model with unrealistic values.

\paragraph{Reward.} The reward for bus $i$ transitioning from stop $k$ to stop $k+1$ is
\[
r_i^{(k)} = - \underbrace{\big(|h^{-}_{i,k+1} - \bar{h}| + |h^{+}_{i,k+1} - \bar{h}|\big)}_{\text{headway deviation}} \;-\; \lambda_1 t_{\text{hold}} \;-\; \lambda_2 \cdot \tanh(a_{\text{speed}}),
\]
where $\bar{h}$ is the target headway for Line 7X derived from its timetable, $\lambda_1, \lambda_2 > 0$ are control-cost weights, and $h^{-}, h^{+}$ are headways to the preceding and following buses on the same line. The first term encourages headway uniformity; the $\lambda_1$ term discourages excessive holding; the $\lambda_2 \tanh$ term penalizes aggressive speed modifications. We use the same $(\lambda_1, \lambda_2)$ values validated in our prior SAC experiments on this environment.

\paragraph{Asynchronous transitions.} Because different buses arrive at stations at different wall-clock times, transitions are \emph{asynchronous}: each bus has its own transition stream. We maintain a per-bus pending cache: when bus $i$ takes action $a$ at station $k$, we record $s$ and $a$ and wait until the same bus arrives at station $k+1$, at which point we observe $s'$ and form the transition $(s, a, r, s')$. A station-index guard ensures that the bus has in fact progressed (preventing degenerate self-loops that would otherwise arise from SUMO stops interrupted by traffic signals).

\paragraph{Episode.} Each episode runs for 18{,}000 simulator steps (0.5\,s per step, $\approx$ 2.5 operational hours covering the morning or evening peak). Across these 18K steps, the 50 Line 7X buses collectively produce approximately 600--1000 state-action-reward transitions depending on traffic. Other lines' buses, social vehicles, and passengers continue to circulate normally and affect bus progress through congestion, signal delays, and passenger boarding loads.

\subsection{Offline Data}
\label{sec:offline_data}

60{,}000 state-action-reward transitions were collected by running the full Changsha simulator under an internal heuristic holding controller. The heuristic uses a schedule-based rule: hold the bus if it is ahead of schedule, proceed immediately otherwise; speed tier is set from a PID-like correction against the scheduled arrival time of the next stop. This is a ``medium-quality'' dataset: the heuristic is competent but substantially suboptimal, attaining roughly $-9800$ mean episode return under its own operation\,---\,BC's ceiling is therefore near this value by construction. Data spans two operational days' worth of peak-hour simulation to provide state-distribution coverage across traffic regimes. All transitions are stored in a single HDF5 file (\texttt{merged\_all\_v2.h5}) and are loaded into a mixed-replay buffer at training time.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

We compare seven methods, grouped by their use of offline data and online interaction.

\paragraph{Pure Offline.}
\textbf{Behavior Cloning (BC).} A Gaussian policy trained to maximize log-likelihood of offline actions \citep{pomerleau1988alvinn}. \textbf{H2O+ offline.} The offline-only mode of H2O+ \citep{niu2022trust}, which learns a value function $V(s)$ via quantile regression and updates the policy with advantage-weighted regression. We include this because it is the offline algorithm already deployed in our prior work on the same data.

\paragraph{Pure Online.}
\textbf{Online SAC.} Soft Actor-Critic \citep{haarnoja2018soft} trained from a random initialization by interacting with the SUMO environment. Uses a single Q-pair, automatic entropy tuning, UTD $=1$.

\paragraph{Offline-to-Online.}
\textbf{WSRL.} Warm-start from a pre-trained offline policy, then discard offline data for online SAC fine-tuning \citep{zhou2024efficient}. Our warm-start checkpoint is the 60K-step H2O+ offline-only policy. \textbf{RLPD.} Random initialization; at every gradient step, draw half the batch from the offline buffer and half from the online buffer \citep{ball2023efficient}. We study three offline ratios: 0.25, 0.50, 0.75.

\paragraph{RE-SAC Offline (Ours).}
A purely offline algorithm built on an ensemble of $E=10$ Q-networks using the Vectorized-Linear architecture of REDQ \citep{chen2021randomized}. Let $Q_i(s,a)$ denote the $i$-th ensemble head. Two regularizers are added on top of standard SAC losses:

\textit{Epistemic (LCB pessimism).} The policy loss becomes
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_\pi = -\mathbb{E}_{s \sim \mathcal{D}, a \sim \pi(\cdot|s)} \big[\, \mu_Q(s,a) + \beta\, \sigma_Q(s,a)\, \big] + \beta_{\text{bc}} \mathcal{L}_{\text{BC}},
    \label{eq:lcb}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_Q$ and $\sigma_Q$ are the mean and standard deviation over the ensemble and $\beta = -2$ yields a lower-confidence-bound estimate that penalizes out-of-distribution actions with high epistemic uncertainty. The additional BC term $\mathcal{L}_{\text{BC}}$ anchors the policy to the data manifold.

\textit{Aleatoric ($L_1$ weight norm).} Let $w_i$ denote the flattened weights of the $i$-th ensemble head. Define $\rho_i = \|w_i\|_1$; the Q-loss includes an explicit penalty
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_Q = \text{MSE}(Q_i(s,a), y) + \alpha_{\text{ood}} \sigma_Q(s,a) + \lambda_{\text{reg}} \sum_{i=1}^{E} \rho_i,
\end{equation}
and the TD target is shifted by the detached $\lambda_{\text{reg}}\rho_i$ for consistency. This regularization is inspired by RNAC-style network-complexity penalties used for aleatoric uncertainty calibration.

Compared to RORL \citep{yang2022rorl}, which uses ensemble LCB \emph{and} smoothness penalties on Q-function inputs, RE-SAC replaces input smoothness with parameter-space $L_1$ regularization. Our ablation in Sec.~\ref{sec:ablation} shows that the LCB pessimism term is the dominant contributor, while the $L_1$ weight-norm penalty is neutral on its own and slightly harmful when combined with LCB. We report results for the full RE-SAC (LCB + $L_1$) in the main comparison for consistency with our original design, but the simpler ``ensemble + LCB'' variant is in fact the stronger recipe. Algorithm~\ref{alg:resac} summarizes the training loop.

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\DontPrintSemicolon
\KwIn{offline dataset $\mathcal{D}$, ensemble size $E$, discount $\gamma$, LCB weight $\beta$, OOD std weight $\alpha_{\text{ood}}$, $L_1$ weight $\lambda_{\text{reg}}$, BC weight $\beta_{\text{bc}}$}
\KwOut{policy $\pi_\phi$}
Initialize $E$ Q-networks $\{Q_{\theta_i}\}_{i=1}^{E}$ using Vectorized-Linear layers, target copies $\{\bar Q_{\theta_i}\}$, policy $\pi_\phi$, entropy $\alpha$. \;
\For{step $=1,\ldots,T$}{
    Sample minibatch $\{(s, a, r, s', d)\} \sim \mathcal{D}$ of size $B$ \;
    \tcp{— Critic update —}
    $\rho_i \leftarrow \|\theta_i^{(w,b)}\|_1$ for each ensemble head $i$  \tcp*[r]{aleatoric L1 norm}
    $a' \sim \pi_\phi(\cdot | s')$; \quad $\mathrm{lp}' \leftarrow \log\pi_\phi(a'|s')$ \;
    With $\nabla_\theta$ stop-gradient: \;
    \quad $y_i \leftarrow \bar Q_{\theta_i}(s', a') - \alpha\,\mathrm{lp}' + \lambda_{\text{reg}}\rho_i$ \;
    \quad $y \leftarrow r + \gamma(1-d)\,\bar y$ where $\bar y = \frac{1}{E}\sum_i y_i$ \tcp*[r]{target} \;
    $\widehat Q_i \leftarrow Q_{\theta_i}(s, a)$ for $i=1,\ldots,E$ \;
    $\sigma_Q \leftarrow \mathrm{std}_i\,\widehat Q_i$ \;
    $\mathcal{L}_Q \leftarrow \frac{1}{E}\sum_i (\widehat Q_i - y)^2 + \alpha_{\text{ood}}\cdot\sigma_Q + \lambda_{\text{reg}}\sum_i \rho_i$ \;
    Update $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}_Q$ \;
    \tcp{— Policy update (LCB pessimism + BC anchor) —}
    $a^\pi \sim \pi_\phi(\cdot|s)$; \quad $\mathrm{lp} \leftarrow \log\pi_\phi(a^\pi|s)$ \;
    $\mu_Q, \sigma_Q \leftarrow$ mean and std of $\{Q_{\theta_i}(s, a^\pi) - \alpha \mathrm{lp}\}$ over $i$ \;
    $\mathcal{L}_\pi \leftarrow -\mathbb{E}[\mu_Q + \beta\,\sigma_Q] \;+\; \beta_{\text{bc}}\,\mathrm{MSE}(a^\pi,\, a_{\text{data}})$ \;
    Update $\phi \leftarrow \phi - \eta \nabla_\phi \mathcal{L}_\pi$ \;
    Automatic $\alpha$ update and soft target sync: $\bar\theta \leftarrow \tau\theta + (1-\tau)\bar\theta$ \;
}
\caption{RE-SAC offline training}
\label{alg:resac}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experimental Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}

\paragraph{Training.} For each method and each of three seeds ($\{42, 123, 456\}$): offline methods train for 100K gradient steps; online and offline-to-online methods for 300 epochs (each epoch = 1 sim rollout of 18K steps + $N_{\text{update}}$ gradient updates). Every 5K steps (offline) or 50 epochs (online), we save a checkpoint.

\paragraph{Evaluation.} \emph{All results in this paper use post-hoc evaluation}: each saved checkpoint is reloaded and evaluated on 10 independent SUMO episodes (with reset seed varied), yielding $\text{mean} \pm \text{std}$ of episode return. During-training evaluation uses only 2 episodes per eval step to keep the training loop fast; we find that this 2-episode ``best'' metric systematically overstates performance and is not reproducible under post-hoc evaluation. Across 151 evaluated checkpoints, with 10 episodes each, this protocol consumed $\sim$2.5~hours on a 14-worker CPU pool.

\paragraph{Metrics.} We report (i) \textbf{Best}: the max mean-return over all checkpoints in a run, averaged across seeds; and (ii) \textbf{Final}: the mean-return at the last checkpoint, averaged across seeds. ``Best'' is the stronger metric from the practitioner's view (you would deploy the best checkpoint); ``Final'' measures whether the method has converged.

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports the main comparison. Three results stand out.

\paragraph{(R1) Naive offline methods fail and overfit.} Both BC and the H2O+ offline-only baseline reach a much worse final return than offline-to-online methods. BC's final ($-9439$) is theoretically bounded by the data-generating heuristic's performance ($\sim$$-9800$), and our BC reproduces this. H2O+ offline is similarly bounded at its final ($-9482$). More strikingly, Figure~\ref{fig:curves} (left panel) shows that both methods exhibit clear \emph{overfitting}: H2O+ offline peaks near step 20--30K at $-6561$ and then degrades monotonically to $-9482$ over the remaining 70K steps; BC peaks earlier at $-8440$ and also degrades. Neither method's ``best'' checkpoint is useful in practice because it would require online SUMO rollouts to identify\,---\,the kind of interaction these methods are supposed to avoid.

\paragraph{(R2) RE-SAC offline is the best overall.} Without any online interaction, RE-SAC attains $-4267 \pm 140$ (best) and $-4520 \pm 269$ (final), surpassing all offline-to-online methods by 5--10\%. Its seed variance ($\sigma = 140$) is also the smallest, an order of magnitude below BC's ($\sigma = 993$) and well below Online SAC's ($\sigma = 421$ best, $292$ final). Crucially, unlike BC and H2O+ offline, RE-SAC \emph{does not overfit}: its curve in Figure~\ref{fig:curves} plateaus by step 20K and remains stable through step 100K. This means any checkpoint past step 20K is deployable without having to identify a ``best'' one\,---\,the deployability property we called out as missing from BC and H2O+ offline in (R1). This is consistent with the interpretation that LCB pessimism prevents the Q-function from exploding on out-of-distribution actions, while the $L_1$ weight regularization keeps individual ensemble heads simple enough that aleatoric uncertainty is well-calibrated.

\paragraph{(R3) Offline-to-online methods form a tight cluster.} WSRL, RLPD with $\rho \in \{0.50, 0.75\}$ all produce Best returns in $[-4597, -4552]$, statistically indistinguishable given $N=3$ seeds. RLPD with $\rho = 0.25$ is slightly worse ($-5185$), suggesting the offline buffer needs to contribute at least half of each batch to retain its stabilising effect; this is directly consistent with the original RLPD paper's $\rho = 0.50$ default \citep{ball2023efficient}. Crucially, none of these offline-to-online methods reach RE-SAC's level, even though they have access to both offline data \emph{and} online rollouts.

Figure~\ref{fig:curves} confirms the table-level findings. RE-SAC's curve is almost flat from step $\sim$20K onward, suggesting that further offline training does not help once the ensemble uncertainty has calibrated. The offline-to-online methods (WSRL, RLPD) reach their plateau around epoch 100 and remain stable. Online SAC's curve, by contrast, fluctuates by several hundred between consecutive checkpoints, indicative of value-function instability rather than lack of exploration. The left panel also makes the difference between \emph{uncertainty-regularized} and \emph{un-regularized} offline RL stark: RE-SAC's plateau vs.\ BC/H2O+'s continuous degradation is a direct visualisation of the value of the LCB $+$ $L_1$ regularizers we introduce.

\subsection{Extended Online SAC: Does Budget Matter?}
\label{sec:sac600}

To check whether the pure-online baseline is simply under-trained, we ran Online SAC on seed 42 for 600 epochs (double the default budget). The single best checkpoint reached $-4998$ at epoch 280\,---\,close to RLPD and WSRL levels\,---\,but the policy then catastrophically regressed to $-6900$ by epoch 300 and oscillated in the range $[-7400, -5400]$ for the remaining 300 epochs. No epoch after 280 ever recovered to the $-5000$ range under post-hoc $N=10$ eval. We conclude that the SAC ``best'' metric under extended training is an ephemeral peak, not a stable solution\,---\,you cannot deploy a checkpoint you identified only post-hoc. In contrast, RE-SAC's best and final differ by only 253 return (Table~\ref{tab:main}), meaning any checkpoint past step 20K is deployable.

\subsection{Ablation: which regularizer matters?}
\label{sec:ablation}

To disentangle the contribution of each regularizer in RE-SAC offline, we run the algorithm with (i) no LCB pessimism ($\beta=0$, keeping $L_1$ reg), (ii) no $L_1$ weight penalty ($\lambda_{\text{reg}}=0$, keeping LCB $\beta=-2$), and (iii) neither regularizer (pure ensemble SAC on offline data). All ablations use the same ensemble size $E=10$ and BC anchor, so the only thing changed is the regularizer under test. Each variant is trained for 100K steps with checkpoint saving every 10K steps, and evaluated post-hoc on $N=10$ SUMO episodes per checkpoint across three seeds.

Three observations follow from Table~\ref{tab:ablation} and Figure~\ref{fig:ablation_curves}. \emph{First}, even the ``ensemble only'' variant\,---\,which has neither LCB pessimism nor $L_1$ weight regularization\,---\,already attains $-4303$, better than every offline-to-online method in Table~\ref{tab:main}. The ensemble-Q architecture with ten heads appears to be the dominant source of RE-SAC's performance gain; the two regularizers studied here provide at most a small additional improvement on top. \emph{Second}, \textbf{LCB pessimism is the single most valuable ingredient}: adding LCB alone yields the strongest result ($-4157 \pm 40$), a clean 150-return improvement over ensemble-only, and also the smallest seed variance (4\% of the mean). \emph{Third}, the $L_1$ weight-norm penalty is not necessary in this offline setting: $L_1$ alone is statistically indistinguishable from ensemble-only ($-4329$ vs.\ $-4303$), and combining $L_1$ with LCB actually performs \emph{worse} than LCB alone ($-4267$ vs.\ $-4157$) while increasing seed variance ($\sigma=140$ vs.\ $40$). We therefore recommend the simpler ``ensemble + LCB'' variant for practitioners; the full RE-SAC configuration reported in Table~\ref{tab:main} was our initial choice for the main comparison, but the honest conclusion is that the $L_1$ term can be dropped without loss.

\paragraph{Caveat on the $L_1$ term.} Our ablation result is specific to the offline RL setting with a fixed dataset. In prior work by the same authors on \emph{online} SUMO bus control, a vanilla ensemble SAC variant without the $L_1$ penalty performed worse than with $L_1$, suggesting that aleatoric weight-norm regularization does help calibrate ensemble uncertainty under continuously shifting on-policy data. We thus keep $L_1$ in Algorithm~\ref{alg:resac} for documentation and future reuse, while noting that for pure offline training the term can be safely removed.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why does offline RL win here?} The bus-holding task has two structural properties that favor good offline methods. \emph{First}, the offline data, while suboptimal, covers the relevant state distribution well: the heuristic controller drives buses across all headway regimes (bunching, spacing, catch-up). An uncertainty-aware offline algorithm can therefore extract a better policy from the dataset than the data-generating policy itself, a regime sometimes called ``offline policy improvement''. \emph{Second}, online exploration is expensive and noisy: each episode is 18K sim-steps over a 12-line network, and the reward signal is dominated by sporadic bunching events. SAC struggles to obtain reliable Q-targets under this SNR, leading to the instability in Sec.~\ref{sec:sac600}. RE-SAC sidesteps this entirely.

\paragraph{When would offline-to-online still pay?} If the deployment environment is \emph{systematically different} from the offline data distribution\,---\,e.g., a new bus route or a post-construction traffic pattern\,---\,then offline-only methods cannot adapt, and the offline-to-online fine-tuning stage recovers its raison d'\^etre. Our benchmark uses the same SUMO environment for data collection and evaluation, which is the best case for offline methods; a domain-shift benchmark is an interesting next step.

\paragraph{Limitations.} (i) Three seeds is modest for establishing statistical significance, though the RE-SAC vs.\ RLPD gap (285 return at $\sigma \approx 300$) is near the boundary of significance; a five-seed replication would strengthen the claim. (ii) We fix the reward scale throughout; sensitivity to reward engineering is untested. (iii) The 600-epoch SAC study was run for one seed only; a three-seed extension would be needed for a definitive conclusion on SAC's long-run behaviour.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have shown that on a realistic SUMO bus-holding benchmark, a properly regularized pure-offline RL method (RE-SAC) outperforms two leading offline-to-online approaches (WSRL, RLPD) without any online environment interaction. Naive offline methods (BC, H2O+ AWR) and pure-online SAC both fall well short. Our ablation reveals that the effective recipe is remarkably simple: \emph{an ensemble of ten Q-networks trained with LCB pessimism} is sufficient; the $L_1$ weight-norm regularization we initially added does not help and can slightly hurt. This yields both low variance across seeds ($\sigma=40$ for the LCB-only variant) and stable convergence: any checkpoint past 20K offline steps is deployable. These findings suggest that practitioners working with operational transit data should consider deploying offline RL directly, without the engineering overhead of staged offline-to-online fine-tuning.

Code, data, checkpoints, and evaluation results (151 post-hoc evaluated checkpoints) are available at \url{https://github.com/erzhu419/offline-sumo}.

\section{Network Architecture}
\label{app:arch}

All methods share a common policy/critic backbone:

\paragraph{Categorical embedding layer.} The 5 categorical state features (line, bus, station, time-period, direction) are embedded separately and concatenated. Embedding table sizes: line (12 $\to$ 4), bus (389 $\to$ 8), station (1 $\to$ 2), time-period (1 $\to$ 2), direction (2 $\to$ 2). LayerNorm ($\epsilon = 10^{-5}$) is applied after concatenation; a 5\% dropout is applied during training. The embedding output (dim $= 18$) is concatenated with the 12 continuous state features to form a state vector of dim $30$.

\paragraph{Policy network (actor).} Two hidden layers of width 48 with ReLU activations, producing a Tanh-Gaussian distribution: mean head outputs $\mu \in \mathbb{R}^2$, log-std head outputs $\log\sigma \in [-5, 2]$, action sampled as $a = \tanh(\mu + \sigma \odot \epsilon)$ with $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$.

\paragraph{Q-network (critic).} Two hidden layers of width 48 with ReLU. For methods using a twin Q (H2O+, SAC, WSRL, RLPD), two independent Q-networks are maintained. For RE-SAC, ten ensemble heads are implemented using a Vectorized-Linear layer that batches all heads in one tensor operation; the forward pass outputs an $E \times B \times 1$ Q-value tensor.

\paragraph{Value network (H2O+ only).} Single hidden layer of width 48 with ReLU; H2O+ uses $V(s)$ for advantage estimation in AWR.

\paragraph{Parameter counts.}
\begin{itemize}
\itemsep0em
\item Policy: 6{,}178 parameters
\item Twin Q: 7{,}586 parameters
\item Ensemble Q (10 heads, Vectorized-Linear): 37{,}970 parameters
\item V-network (H2O+): 3{,}697 parameters
\item Total embedding table: 4{,}012 parameters (shared across actor \& critic, separate clones for each net)
\end{itemize}

\section{Reproducibility}
\label{app:repro}

\paragraph{Seeds.} All results are averaged over three seeds $\{42, 123, 456\}$. Each training script sets PyTorch and NumPy RNG seeds at startup. The SUMO simulator seed is governed internally by the route-file stochastic demand sampling and is \emph{not} set by our code; this yields genuine traffic-level stochasticity across evaluation episodes rather than deterministic replays.

\paragraph{Compute environment.} Training was performed on a dual-GPU server (2 $\times$ NVIDIA RTX with 12 GiB each, 12 CPU cores, Ubuntu 22.04, Python 3.8, PyTorch 2.4.1 + CUDA 12.1). Each training script fits entirely in $<$ 1 GiB of GPU memory, so we ran 3 seeds of a method concurrently per GPU. Post-hoc evaluation ran on a separate 16-core CPU workstation (no GPU needed since SUMO is the bottleneck); a 14-worker \texttt{multiprocessing} pool evaluated 151 checkpoints in $\sim$2.5 hours.

\paragraph{Wall-clock costs.} Training wall-clock per 100K-step run, measured as elapsed time when run 3-in-parallel on one GPU:
\begin{itemize}
\itemsep0em
\item BC: $\sim$30\,min
\item H2O+ offline: $\sim$60\,min
\item RE-SAC offline: $\sim$60\,min
\item Online SAC (300 epochs): $\sim$1.5\,h (dominated by SUMO rollouts)
\item WSRL (300 epochs): $\sim$2\,h
\item RLPD (300 epochs): $\sim$2\,h
\end{itemize}
Post-hoc evaluation: $\sim$1\,min per SUMO episode, so a checkpoint's $N=10$ evaluation takes $\sim$10\,min on one CPU core.

\section{Data Format}
\label{app:data}

The offline dataset \texttt{merged\_all\_v2.h5} is a single HDF5 file with the following keys: \texttt{observations} ($N \times 17$, float32), \texttt{actions} ($N \times 2$, float32, in the raw tanh range $[-1,1]$), \texttt{rewards} ($N$, float32), \texttt{next\_observations} ($N \times 17$), \texttt{dones} ($N$, bool), and \texttt{contexts} ($N \times 30$, unused in this paper but retained for compatibility with context-conditioned policies from prior work). The \texttt{BusMixedReplayBuffer} loader normalizes continuous observation dimensions to zero mean and unit variance using statistics computed once at load time and cached for evaluation.
